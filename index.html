MergeMapp:


A Mobile Travel App Case Study

This project is a part of the UX/UI Bootcamp at the University of Toronto, we were given 4 weeks to create a mobile travel app from scratch and this is what I came up with

Research


Due to the recent pandemic, people around the world were required to remain in their respective countries, cities, and even their own home therefore no one was allowed to travel for quite some time. But since travel restrictions have started to lift, people now more than ever are eager to start planning the trips they had hoped to go on or have aspired to go on since the lockdown. I conducted five 1-on-1 interviews with frequent travellers in Toronto to kick off the user research process and supplemented it with a competitor analysis focusing on what their main features are and what is missing from them.

Definition & Ideation


Using the information from my user research, I developed the user persona: an adventure-seeking young professional who enjoys travelling. Their pain points include having limited time to explore a city to its full potential and having to flip through multiple apps for coordinating bookings and itinerary.

The problem then became apparent, there was a need to communicate more efficiently amongst tripmates regarding plans which requires juggling between multiple apps. There were several ideas to solve this issue.

To solve this issue, the goal of this project focused on reducing the stress and time it takes to plan group trips by having the users share their own interests with their friends and compiling a group map/itinerary with everyone’s pins. Doing this will allow the product to be a hub for travel groups and make planning trips, prior to and during, more efficient.

Prototyping


Now for the designing of the app, I sketched up screens pertaining to the app’s main features being searching and pinning locations on a map, a compiled list of pinned locations showing who pinned what, the group chat, and personal profile with a list of created trips, along with the login/signup screens.



Using the rough sketches and digital wireframes, I created a lo-fi prototype which I used to conduct a usability test with the objective of making sure the user flow, buttons, and navigation were seamless and intuitive. I recruited five participants to complete five simple tasks on the clickable lo-fi prototype which can be seen below.

Iterations


Based on the feedback from the usability tests, some of the iterations that were made included various micro interactions like swipes and pull downs to go back and minimize as well as the following:

added an error message if someone tried to create a trip without naming it

changed the button for adding a pin on the map so that it is less ambiguous

auto-generating results as one types in the search bar

MergeMapp lets everyone lead the way.


This app allows you and your friends to plan trips together hassle-free. Set up a shared map, place pins, share locations, and have fun.

What I Learned


Having been my first design challenge and case study, I learned so much about every step of the design process and how each phase contributes to the final prototype. These are the most notable things I’ve learned:

Keep It simple:
When developing a product, you will need to either scrap or put on hold many ideas and really focus on one or two main functions. Honing in on solving one or two problems will really help you narrow down your ideas.

Observational and soft-skills are crucial:
When conducting user interviews or usability tests, having good observational skills was extremely useful as it gave me a deeper understanding of how a user feels or thinks beyond what they say. I also found that during these phases, having the soft-skills to make participants feel comfortable and relaxed allowed for more natural, expansive and in-depth answers which provided me with crucial information that carried through to the next steps.

Don’t be offended by feedback:
Feedback is an essential part of every step of the design process. Regardless of whether it comes from users or colleagues, or if it was gentle or harsh, all feedback can be useful.

The next steps for MergeMapp would be to perhaps add a feature that allows users to favourite and share/recommend the trips they’ve been on with other users as well as specific locations that they really enjoyed. This would expand the purpose and usefulness of the app allowing for more browsability and extend its planning resources.
